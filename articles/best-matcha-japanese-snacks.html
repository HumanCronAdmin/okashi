Introduction

Matcha is Japan's most iconic flavor. From KitKat to Pocky, matcha-flavored snacks have taken the world by storm. But which ones are actually worth trying? Here's our curated guide to the best matcha Japanese snacks, based on real taste-testing in Japan.

The Best Matcha Snacks

KitKat Mini Matcha (キットカット ミニ 抹茶)

Ah, the ubiquitous KitKat. In Japan, it's more than just a chocolate bar; it's a souvenir staple and a gateway to countless regional flavors. The Matcha Mini version is a classic for a reason. The bright green coating, made with real Uji matcha, delivers a wonderfully earthy and slightly bitter punch that perfectly cuts through the sweetness of the white chocolate base. It’s not an overpowering matcha flavor, but a sophisticated whisper that lingers pleasantly.

The texture is exactly what you expect from a KitKat: a satisfying snap from the crisp wafer layers, followed by the smooth melt of the chocolate coating. I love grabbing a few of these when I’m on the go, especially during a long train ride. They’re perfect with a cup of unsweetened sencha, as the tea's natural astringency complements the KitKat's delicate balance of bitter and sweet. You’ll find these everywhere, from convenience stores to airport gift shops.

Pocky Matcha Green Tea (ポッキー 抹茶)

Glico's Pocky is another legend in the Japanese snack world, and their Matcha Green Tea flavor is a sophisticated evolution of the original. Unlike some overly sweet matcha treats, this Pocky offers a more refined, almost artisanal matcha experience. The pretzel sticks are coated in a generous layer of matcha chocolate that boasts a beautiful, deep green hue and an aroma that hints at its quality. The flavor is a delightful dance between the subtle sweetness and the distinct, slightly grassy bitterness of good matcha.

The crunch of the pretzel is always a highlight, providing a sturdy base for the smooth, creamy matcha coating. It’s the kind of snack that makes you slow down and savor each bite. I often enjoy these in the late afternoon with a glass of cold milk, or even with a small shot of shochu for a more adult pairing. They’re readily available in most supermarkets and even smaller neighborhood grocery stores across Japan.

Meiji Milk Chocolate Matcha (明治ミルクチョコレート抹茶)

Meiji’s Milk Chocolate is a childhood favorite for many in Japan, and their foray into matcha is a testament to the flavor's popularity. This isn't a fancy, individually wrapped bar, but a simple, classic milk chocolate bar infused with matcha. The result is a surprisingly harmonious blend. The creamy, familiar sweetness of Meiji’s milk chocolate is present, but it’s beautifully complemented by a gentle, verdant matcha undertone. It’s less about a sharp matcha kick and more about a smooth, integrated flavor profile.

The texture is exactly as you’d expect from a quality milk chocolate bar: smooth, melts easily on the tongue, and has that satisfying snap when you break off a piece. I find this bar to be incredibly versatile. It’s perfect for a quick desk snack during a busy workday, or even crumbled over a bowl of vanilla ice cream for an instant matcha-infused dessert. You can easily find these in the chocolate aisle of any Japanese supermarket or convenience store.

Bourbon Alfort Matcha (ブルボン アルフォート 抹茶)

Bourbon's Alfort biscuits are a delightful combination of a crisp, slightly sweet biscuit base and a layer of chocolate. Their Matcha version takes this winning formula and elevates it with the earthy notes of green tea. The biscuit itself has a lovely, subtle sweetness that provides a perfect counterpoint to the rich, dark chocolate and the distinct matcha flavor. It’s a well-balanced snack where no single element overpowers the others, creating a truly satisfying bite.

The texture is a wonderful contrast: the firm, crumbly biscuit giving way to the smooth, slightly melty chocolate. It’s a satisfying crunch that’s not too hard and not too soft. I often find myself reaching for an Alfort Matcha with my morning coffee; the slight bitterness of the coffee really brings out the nuances of the matcha. These are a common sight in supermarkets and even in larger convenience stores, often found in the biscuit or cookie section.

Glico Giant Pocky Matcha (グリコ ジャンボポッキー 抹茶)

For those who love Pocky but want a more substantial matcha experience, the Giant Pocky in Matcha is a fantastic option. It’s essentially a larger, thicker version of the classic Pocky, offering more of that delightful matcha-coated goodness. The matcha chocolate is generous, providing a more intense flavor profile than its smaller counterpart. You get a robust, earthy matcha taste that’s still beautifully balanced with the sweetness, making it feel like a more grown-up, indulgent treat.

The satisfying crunch is amplified with these larger sticks, making each bite feel more substantial. The thicker coating also means a creamier, more luxurious melt in your mouth. I enjoy these as a special treat when I’m relaxing at home, perhaps with a book and a cup of hojicha (roasted green tea), which has a nutty flavor that pairs wonderfully with the matcha. You can usually find these in larger supermarkets or specialty snack stores.

How to Choose the Right One

When you're faced with a shelf full of matcha goodness, consider what you're in the mood for. If you want a light, accessible introduction to matcha snacks, the KitKat Mini Matcha is a safe and delicious bet. For a more refined, slightly bitter profile, the classic Pocky Matcha Green Tea is your go-to. If you prefer a smoother integration of flavors, where matcha is a subtle enhancement rather than the star, the Meiji Milk Chocolate Matcha or Bourbon Alfort Matcha are excellent choices. And for a more indulgent, intense matcha hit, the Giant Pocky Matcha delivers.

Where to Buy These Snacks

The beauty of Japanese snacks, especially popular ones like these matcha treats, is their accessibility. You'll find them readily available in almost any convenience store (konbini) or supermarket across Japan. For a wider selection, including limited edition or regional variations, larger department stores and specialty snack shops are your best bet. If you're outside of Japan, don't despair!
