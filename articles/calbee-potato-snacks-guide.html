how many things can you do with a potato?

calbee asked that question and then spent decades answering it. sticks in a cup. thick-cut chips that fight back when you bite them. shrimp crackers that aren't even potato but somehow feel like they belong. whole-cut fries in a box. all from the same company, all completely different textures, all in every konbini in the country for under ¥200.

i go through calbee stuff faster than anything else in my pantry. there's always a jagariko cup on my desk and a bag of kappa ebisen somewhere near the couch. they're just part of the furniture at this point.

the lineup

jagariko salad flavor (じゃがりこ サラダ)

pop the lid, pull out a stick, bite. the crunch is loud and dry — like a crispy french fry but with no grease on your fingers. there are tiny flecks of parsley and carrot in there, which sounds odd but adds a fresh herby edge. the cup design means you can eat these with one hand while you type with the other. i do this every day. my coworkers know the sound.

here's something people do: pour hot water into an empty jagariko cup with a few sticks left, wait two minutes, and you get instant mashed potato. calbee doesn't officially recommend this but everyone does it.

jagariko cheese (じゃがりこ チーズ)

same crunch, different mood. the cheese flavor is richer than salad — more of an evening snack than an afternoon one. i reach for these when i'm on the couch watching something. the cheese isn't overpowering, more of a warm savory thing. my partner eats the salad, i eat the cheese, and we trade sticks back and forth without thinking about it. that's the kind of snack jagariko is.

calbee kata-age chips lightly salted (堅あげポテト うすしお)

these chips fight back. the name means "hard-fried" and you can hear someone eating them from the next room. thick cut, dense, with a crunch that takes real jaw effort. the flavor is just potato and salt — nothing else. that simplicity is the point. i grab a bag on friday evenings with a beer and that combination hasn't gotten old in years.

calbee kappa ebisen (かっぱえびせん)

open the bag and smell it — a faint ocean breeze. these are light, airy, and made with real shrimp. they've been around since 1964. the tagline is "you can't stop eating them" and it's been on the bag for 60 years because it's still true. i brought a bag to a picnic once and a friend who'd never had them looked at me like i'd been keeping a secret. the bag was gone in minutes.

jagabee lightly salted (Jagabee うすしお)

jagariko's more natural cousin. these are cut from whole potatoes so the shape is a little irregular, the flavor is more earthy, more potato-forward. the crunch is different too — more hollow, a little gentler. i eat these on the balcony on nice days. they feel simpler, more honest. less snack, more potato.

quick comparison

snack texture sweetness (1-5) best moment konbini price (approx)
jagariko salad very crunchy, dry 1 waiting for train, desk work ¥150
jagariko cheese very crunchy, dry 2 evening relaxation, watching tv ¥150
kata-age lightly salted thick, hard crunch 1 desk snack, simple craving ¥130
kappa ebisen light, airy crisp 1 casual sharing, on-the-go ¥120
jagabee lightly salted crunchy, slightly hollow 1 balcony chill, mild craving ¥150

which one first?

if you like a strong, savory flavor and a really loud crunch, go for jagariko cheese. if you prefer something lighter and a bit herby, the jagariko salad is your best bet. for a no-nonsense, thick potato chip, kata-age is the way to go.

where to find them

you can find all of these at pretty much any konbini (7-eleven, family mart, lawson), supermarkets, and even drugstores across japan. they're also usually available on amazon japan if you're looking to stock up.
