Kit Kats vs. Tokyo Banana: Which Japanese Snack Wins?

Updated April 14, 2026

Kit Kats aren’t just chocolate bars in Japan

They’re a seasonal experience. The first time I tried matcha Kit Kat in Kyoto, I understood why they’re everywhere—from vending machines to department store gift sets. The packaging alone feels like a souvenir. Even the plain milk chocolate version comes in a glossy purple wrapper with kanji that changes with the season. It’s thoughtful, almost like a tiny art project.

The texture is what surprises people. Japanese Kit Kats aren’t as crumbly as the ones abroad. The layers of chocolate and wafer hold together better, almost like a thin, delicate cookie. The matcha version isn’t bitter—it’s bright, almost vegetal, with a faint sweetness that lingers. The strawberry flavor tastes like real fruit, not syrup. It’s closer to the syrup from a strawberry shortcake than the artificial taste of Western versions.

Prices vary. A single bar costs ¥150 to ¥200 at convenience stores. Airport gift sets run ¥1,000 to ¥1,500 for five bars. The seasonal flavors, like sake-lees Kit Kat for New Year’s, sell out fast. If you see one, buy it. You won’t find it again.

Tokyo Banana is a different kind of obsession

It’s not just a snack. It’s a Tokyo souvenir wrapped in nostalgia. The melon pan–shaped sponge cake is soft, almost marshmallow-like inside, with a thin, crisp shell that cracks when you press it. The filling isn’t just melon-flavored—it’s made with real melon puree, which gives it a subtle tartness that balances the sugar. The texture is what sets it apart. It’s not dry like most sponge cakes. It’s moist, almost like biting into a cloud that’s been lightly sweetened.

The packaging is iconic: pastel pink with a cartoon rabbit that’s been part of the design since the 1990s. The original Tokyo Banana sells for ¥180 to ¥250 in Tokyo Station or Don Quijote. The limited editions, like matcha or strawberry, cost ¥300 to ¥400. They’re sold in train stations, which makes them easy to grab as a gift. But beware—the melon pan version is a different product entirely. It’s a plain sponge cake shaped like a melon pan, with no filling. People confuse the two all the time.

Common mistake: Assuming all Kit Kats in Japan taste the same

The matcha Kit Kat isn’t just green chocolate. The amount of matcha powder varies by brand and season. Some versions, like the ones from Shizuoka, use high-quality ceremonial-grade matcha. Others taste like artificial flavoring. The strawberry Kit Kat from Hokkaido uses local strawberries, so it’s sweeter and more fragrant than the regular version. If you buy a random matcha Kit Kat, you might end up with a mediocre one. The key is to check the region or the kanji on the wrapper. If it says 「抹茶」 without any extra characters, it’s a safe bet.

Another mistake is buying Tokyo Banana outside of Tokyo. The original is only sold in Tokyo Station or select shops in the city. The versions you find in airports or online are often imitations or different products altogether. The melon pan–shaped sponge cake is a local specialty, not a national one. If you want the real thing, you have to go to Tokyo—or order from a reputable online store that sources directly from Tokyo Banana’s factory.

Common mistake: Thinking Tokyo Banana is always melon-flavored

The original Tokyo Banana is melon-flavored, but the brand has expanded to matcha, strawberry, and even cheese flavors. The cheese version is savory, almost like a cream cheese-filled bun. It’s polarizing. People either love it or hate it. The matcha version is a safe choice, but it’s not the same as the original. The strawberry version is sweet but lacks the tartness of the melon puree. The key is to stick to the original if you want the classic experience. Anything else is an experiment.

And then there’s the melon pan itself. The melon pan–shaped sponge cake is a different product from the filled Tokyo Banana. It’s sold in bakeries and convenience stores, often under different brands. It’s not the same thing. If you’re looking for the iconic Tokyo souvenir, you want the filled version with the rabbit on the wrapper.

Pro tip: How to get the best versions without overpaying

For Kit Kats, the best place to buy is not the airport. It’s the depachika—department store basements like Mitsukoshi or Takashimaya in Tokyo. They carry regional flavors you won’t find elsewhere. The shelves change with the seasons, so if you see a Hokkaido sweet potato Kit Kat in October, buy it. It’s gone by November. The prices are the same as convenience stores, but the selection is better.

For Tokyo Banana, the best place to buy is the Tokyo Station shop or the official online store. The online store ships internationally, but the prices are high due to shipping. If you’re in Japan, buy it fresh from the station. The cakes are made daily, so they’re always soft. If you buy them online, they might arrive stale. And if you’re buying as a gift, wrap them in the original packaging. The rabbit design is part of the experience.

The melon pan version of Tokyo Banana is a different story. If you want that, buy it from a bakery in Tokyo or from a specialty Japanese snack store like Yamibuy or TokyoTreat. But don’t expect the same quality as the original. The melon pan version is a mass-produced snack, not a premium souvenir.

The verdict: Which one wins?

Kit Kats are for snackers who want variety. Tokyo Banana is for souvenir hunters who want something soft, nostalgic, and uniquely Tokyo. Kit Kats are easy to find and come in endless flavors. Tokyo Banana is a one-trick pony, but it’s a trick that works. The melon puree filling is unlike anything else. It’s not just sweet—it’s complex, like a grown-up version of a childhood treat.

If you’re choosing between the two, it depends on what you want. Do you want a snack that changes with the seasons and fits in your pocket? Get a Kit Kat. Do you want a soft, melty, nostalgic souvenir that screams “I was in Tokyo”? Get a Tokyo Banana. Just don’t mix them up with the melon pan version. That’s a rookie mistake.
